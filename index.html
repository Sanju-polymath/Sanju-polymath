About

I am a live performance professional based in Bangalore — ten years across theatre, festivals, circus, residencies, and new media installations.

My work doesn't sit in one lane. I stage manage, production manage, set design, build things with my hands, teach myself 3D modelling, wire up electronics, and run festivals — sometimes all in the same year, often several roles on the same project. The common thread is that I understand the full lifecycle of a production: how a set is conceived, how it is built, how it reassembles at the venue looking exactly as it should, and how it comes down safely at the end. That end-to-end thinking — simultaneously designer, engineer, and logistician — is what I bring to everything I touch.

Over the past decade I have managed more than 350 live performances across India, beginning at Ranga Shankara in Bangalore and building through QTP Entertainment in Mumbai, Bliss Circus in Goa, large-scale musical productions, and immersive new media work. More recently the practice has expanded into auditorium and spatial design — self-taught, real commissions — and installation art with the One by Zero collective.

I came to all of this through doing, not through formal education. I am shaped by the Khoj Fellowship at Swaraj University, Udaipur — where I learned to design my own learning, choose my own mentors, and stay accountable to the work itself. That approach — follow the genuine curiosity, go deep, figure it out — is still how I operate.

Theatre and live performance has always been fun — and it still is. But somewhere along the way I became very aware of how carbon-heavy this art form is. The construction of the auditorium. The energy it takes to light a performance. The materials a set is built from, and what happens to them after. The fuel burned moving everything from city to city. The sheer consumption that surrounds even a single show. Midway through my career I started consciously shaping my work around these questions — which is part of why the practice looks the way it does: upcycled sculpture, self-taught 3D modelling, alternative materials, community residencies, spaces designed around low-energy futures.

We are watching homes become automated — LED lighting, battery storage, renewable charging, every system controlled through a single interface, appliances rated for efficiency. I believe performance spaces and art galleries are going the same way. And that is the thing I am working toward: a performance space that packs into a truck, travels anywhere, assembles in two to three days, and can host a gathering, a performance, an exhibition — running entirely on batteries charged by solar and other renewable sources. No grid dependency. No permanent footprint. A space that belongs wherever it is needed.

Everything you have seen here is the path to that.

Set Design

Set Designer · Carpentry · 3D Modelling · Upcycling

I chose set designing after years of executing other people's visions — and it made sense because I already knew the craft from the inside: carpentry, electrical work, basic painting, and a strong instinct for upcycling materials into something new. I'm very good with my hands, and I can 3D model and execute — from planning and choosing materials all the way through to the finished build.

A lot of the productions I design for involve children performing on stage, which brings a specific set of safety considerations into the design process — the right wheels, padded corners, what happens if something falls, how to handle it in the moment. These nuances are always part of how I think through a set. I also design for portability: sets that can be transported easily and reassembled with minimal setup time, so the set itself never becomes a burden at venue. I can plan, build by myself, and supervise others to execute my design. These are some of my recent set design works.

Auditorium Design

Auditorium Designer · 3D Modelling · Spatial Design · 2024–25

My entry into auditorium design came entirely from the inside — not from architecture school, but from a decade of working inside performance spaces across India. Touring with productions, stage managing shows in the same auditoriums repeatedly, living inside these spaces — I began to notice, very clearly, how most of them were failing performers and audiences. Resource-heavy concrete construction, fixed seating, single-direction viewing, centralised AC systems, outdated electrical infrastructure. I would close my eyes in the middle of a show and visualise what this space could be — how the audience should move, where the artists should emerge from, where the artifacts and installations should live in between. That spatial intuition — built from thousands of hours inside performance environments — became the foundation of everything I do in design.

When I tried to communicate these ideas to architects and designers, words were not enough. So I taught myself 3D modelling from scratch. No formal training, no course — just experimentation, observation, and practice until I could build what I was seeing in my head. My first major project was a conceptual geodesic dome performance structure: lightweight construction, alternative acoustics, modular staging, DC-powered lighting integrated with a centralised DMX network. A future-ready performance space designed around how technology in live events was actually evolving.

Those visualisations opened doors. Prestige Centre for Performing Arts brought me into their project to convert a banquet hall and kitchen into a flexible, technologically integrated black-box performance venue. I developed concepts and spatial designs for that project in collaboration with Prakash Belawadi. Separately, I also contributed 3D modelling and visualisation work for Belawadi's concepts for Shri Riddhagundi Mahaganapati Yakshagana Mandali — a traditional performance institution brought into contemporary spatial thinking. Both PDF reports are linked below.

The cycle completed when Srishti School of Design invited me to deliver a guest lecture on auditorium and performance space design to their students — passing on the same self-directed, practice-led learning I had built for myself. Learn by doing, figure it out yourself, execute it, then pass it on.

Education & Approach to Work

Swaraj University · Khoj Fellowship · Self-Directed Learning

Since childhood I was deeply active in theatre — acting, directing, building things, running science experiments. Very hands-on, very present in anything that involved doing rather than sitting. Written studies were a different story. I have a reading and writing disability — dyslexia — and for years it quietly eroded my confidence, because I could see that I was doing well in everything else while falling behind in the one thing school valued most. Eventually I stopped. I couldn't complete high school, and I didn't have any ambition to push through it and on to college — because I already understood, clearly, that that wasn't the way I needed to go. I started working young.

Then I discovered Swaraj University. The Khoj Fellowship is a two-year, self-designed learning programme run by Swaraj University in Udaipur — one of India's most radical alternatives to formal higher education. There is no fixed curriculum, no exams, no degrees. Instead, each learner — called a Khoji, meaning seeker — designs their own learning path from scratch: choosing what to study, finding mentors in the real world, taking on internships, and building a practice accountable to themselves and their peers. The programme is built on the belief that genuine learning happens when it is self-owned, self-paced, and grounded in real doing.

For me it was the first time I had a structure that I had actually built myself. I learned how to choose the right mentors — not just find someone willing to teach, but identify who I genuinely needed to learn from and go after them deliberately. I learned the value of peer-to-peer accountability: checking in with people who are also trying to build something, not just with an authority figure above you. And I came to understand accountability to the planet — that we cannot use resources the way we want without consequence, and that building anything responsibly means being mindful of that from the beginning.

We lived on a community organic farm in Udaipur — bananas growing in Rajasthan, which shouldn't happen, but they did. We cooked our own food, maintained our own hostels. There were no cleaners, no wardens, no staff watching over us. We had written our own constitution. We ran our own parliament. A community council changed hands twice a week — so every person cycled through leadership and accountability. The only thing keeping it all together was the agreement we had made with each other and with ourselves. That is what I mean by self-ownership: not independence from others, but genuine ownership of the space and the programme you're part of — because you chose it, and you've agreed to live by what that choice means.

I took everything I learned there and applied it directly to how I work. Multiple internships across the country followed — exploring, experimenting, building range. That practice of self-direction is why I can move across stage management, set design, auditorium design, and installation production without losing the thread. I am still doing multiple things, and I know there is a bigger picture I am weaving them toward. Swaraj is the closest thing I have to a college — and in most of the ways that matter, it was better than any college I know.

Selected Projects

Ronak & Jessie

Technical Coordinator · BookMyShow Live · Dir. Feroz Abbas Khan

Ronak & Jessie was a large-scale Punjabi musical adaptation inspired by Romeo and Juliet, produced by BookMyShow Live and directed by Feroz Abbas Khan. As Technical Coordinator for the Delhi run, my responsibilities began at pre-production — coordinating truck bookings, supervising the loading of all scenic and technical materials from Mumbai storage, and managing the movement of the entire production infrastructure to JLN Auditorium, Delhi. Sets, trussing, lighting systems, costumes, props, and backstage materials all moved across cities under my coordination.

At the venue, the existing theatre infrastructure was unreliable for a production of this scale, so we built a temporary production environment from scratch — independent truss systems for lighting rigging, adapted stage layout, and complete backstage and green room facilities for a large ensemble of actors, dancers, and crew. I coordinated installation teams against CAD drawings and technical plans, and made real-time decisions at every constraint: scenic elements that couldn't fit loading bay entrances were modified and refabricated on-site. After the run, I supervised the full dismantling, careful repacking, and return logistics to Mumbai — ensuring all scenic and prop elements remained reusable for future performances.

Bliss Circus

Stage / Residency Manager · Aerial Rigging · Goa · 2022

At Bliss Circus I worked as Residency Manager while also stage managing live circus performances and supporting the overall functioning of the space. I underwent aerial rigging training and gained practical experience in circus rigging safety, maintenance, and operational procedures. Bliss Circus is an open-air jungle circus in a coastal environment — which gave me first-hand exposure to the challenges of maintaining a performance space constantly exposed to nature: weather, humidity, salt air, rain, sunlight, and organic growth directly affecting equipment and infrastructure over time.

A significant part of my work involved long-term care and preservation of wooden stages, circus rigs, costumes, and apparatus in harsh conditions. I developed natural methods of termite prevention for wooden structures, regularly inspected rigging systems for rust and structural wear from coastal air, and created maintenance routines to ensure safety and longevity. I also worked on preserving costumes and aerial apparatus from mould and fungal growth — using vacuum-sealed storage, dehumidifiers, and protective drum storage through intense monsoon seasons. Living in a treehouse directly opposite the stage deepened my understanding of adaptive living and site-responsive performance practice — being continuously exposed to the same conditions as the infrastructure taught me how to coexist with nature while maintaining a functional and safe performance environment.

Sensistan — Circle 3 Residency & Lost in BLR

Residency Mentor · Production Design · New Media Art · Bangalore Creative Circus · 2024

Circle 3 Residency, hosted by Sensistan, was a new media art residency bringing together artists exploring technology, interaction, and spatial storytelling. I worked as part of the core residency team — contributing across production design, spatial planning, facilitation, and exhibition execution. My role as a mentor in production design drew on my background in spatial design, set design, fabrication, and production management rather than new media art itself. I guided participants in planning installations by considering audience movement, spatial visibility, structural feasibility, material selection, rigging possibilities, projection placement, lighting integration, and overall visual composition — helping artists understand how installations function not just as objects, but as spatial experiences.

A major part of my work involved translating artistic concepts into physically executable installations: advising on fabrication methods, identifying materials, planning mounting systems, determining rigging points, and solving practical technical challenges as they emerged. I was also involved in co-designing the final exhibition space alongside another exhibition designer — spatial planning, placement of works, visitor flow, technical integration, and maintaining a cohesive visual language across multiple artists' works. My involvement spanned the full residency lifecycle: reviewing applications, interviewing and selecting participants, facilitating creative and production processes, and overseeing installation management, maintenance, and dismantling at the end.

Lost in BLR was the month-long exhibition at Bangalore Creative Circus that followed the residency — the same participants' works presented to a public audience, with me overseeing the operational and technical running of the show throughout its run.

Echoes of Earth

Installation Artist · Interactive Installation · India's Leading Sustainable Arts Festival · 2024

At Echoes of Earth — India's leading sustainable arts and music festival — I worked as an installation artist, contributing to multiple interactive installations created collaboratively with Adish and Akash.

Our primary installation drew its concept from the lifecycle of the fig wasp: the mother wasp enters a fig, pollinates it, lays her eggs inside, and in doing so gives up her own life — a quiet act of sacrifice that ensures the survival of the next generation. We depicted this entire cycle as an immersive, interactive experience using LED orbs, CRT televisions, and speakers to build the environment. Visitors could interact with the installation through Leap Motion sensors and custom-built touch sensors we wired together to create our own MIDI controller — essentially a hand-built instrument that let us, and the audience, shape the sound and light in real time.

Alongside this, I fabricated a large-scale wasp sculpture entirely from collected waste materials — a physical representation of the creature at the heart of the concept. We also created a kinetic flower installation and a headboard installation, both of which were interactive, responding to audience presence and touch. All fabrication, electronics housing, and light prototyping were done by hand.

The Sixth Sense Festival — One by Zero

Installation Artist · One by Zero Collective · Swordfish · Bengaluru · February 2026

The Sixth Sense Festival is India's first large-scale multidisciplinary immersive festival — conceived by the team behind Echoes of Earth and curated by Swordfish. It was held at Alembic City, Whitefield, Bengaluru: a 60-year-old glass factory reimagined as a 200,000 sq. ft. arena for art, technology, music, and ecology. Across 18 days in February 2026, it brought together 30+ art-tech experiences, live performances, and installations from artists across India and abroad — all organised around the theme of nature's intelligence and how we might learn from it.

We were among the few Indian artists' collectives invited to present an installation. One by Zero — our collective — came together during the Sensistan Circle 3 Residency, and has kept making things together since. Swordfish invited us to develop and install a work for the festival.

Our installation was built around the spider and its web. A spider's web is not just a trap — it is an extended sensory organ. Every vibration that touches a single thread travels instantly to the spider at the centre: a foreign particle landing on the web, a disturbance in the air, the weight of a raindrop. The web is the spider's nervous system extended outward into space, and the spider is always listening. We translated this phenomenon — the transfer of signal from web to spider and spider back to web — into a series of lights and sound that visitors could feel and interact with, experiencing the sensation of being at the centre of a living, responsive network.
